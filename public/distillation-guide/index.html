Why Models Need to Travel Light

You have trained a transformer with 340 million parametersLearnable weights in a neural network. More parameters generally means more capacity, but also more memory and compute. Learn more →. It achieves state-of-the-art accuracy. It is also 1.3 gigabytes, requires a GPU, and takes 400 milliseconds per prediction. Now someone asks you to run it on a phone.

This is the deployment problem. Cloud inference costs scale with model size. Edge devices have hard memory limits. Latency-sensitive applications, from autocomplete to autonomous driving, cannot wait for a large model to think.

The obvious fix: train a smaller model from scratch. But smaller models trained on the same data almost always underperform. They lack the capacity to learn the same patterns. What if a smaller model could learn not just the right answers, but the reasoning behind them, by studying a larger model that already mastered the task?

That idea is knowledge distillation.

The core idea

The deployment problem is not "how to train smaller models." It is "how to transfer what large models learned into smaller ones without losing what matters."

A Brief History

The idea appeared in Buciluă et al. (2006): a compact network trained to mimic a large ensemble could match its performance. The ensemble's predictions contained richer information than ground-truth labels alone.

Hinton, Vinyals & Dean (2015) formalized this into what we now call knowledge distillation. Their innovation: temperature scaling, which softens the teacher's output to reveal hidden inter-class relationships, what Hinton termed "dark knowledge."

Romero et al. (2015) extended distillation beyond final outputs. Their FitNets framework trained students to match intermediate hidden layer activationsInternal representations at each layer of a neural network. Earlier layers capture low-level features (edges); deeper layers capture abstract concepts (objects, relationships). Learn more →, opening a new axis of knowledge transfer.

The modern era brought distillation to LLMs: DistilBERT (2019) retained 97% of BERT in 40% less space. TinyBERT (2020) pushed further with two-stage distillation. And DeepSeek-R1 (2025) popularized using generated reasoning data to train smaller dense models with strong reasoning behavior.

Evolution

Distillation evolved: copy the output (2006) → copy internal representations (2015) → use generated reasoning data (2025).

The Teacher-Student Setup

Knowledge distillation has two actors. The teacher is a large, pre-trained model. The student is smaller, designed for deployment. In the standard setup, the student learns from both ground-truth labels and the teacher's full probability distribution over classes. That softened distribution supplies extra structure the labels alone do not contain.

Figure 1. The teacher-student architecture. The teacher (BERT-Base, 12 layers) produces softened probability distributions that the student (DistilBERT, 6 layers) learns to match. Both process the same transfer batch, and the student is usually trained on a mix of ground-truth labels and the teacher's softened outputs.

The transfer set need not be the original labeled training set: in practice it can be labeled data, held-out examples, or additional unlabeled data run through the teacher. The teacher and student need not share the same architecture. A 12-layer transformer can teach a 6-layer transformer, or even a CNN. This works for response-based distillation, which only requires matching output distributions. Feature-based distillation across different architectures is harder, since internal representations differ in structure and dimensionality.

But what exactly makes that distribution so valuable? Why is a probability vector better than a simple label? The answer lies in temperature scaling.

Soft Labels and Temperature Scaling

A neural network's final layer produces logitsRaw, unnormalized scores from the final layer. Before softmax, these can be any real number. Higher = model thinks this class is more likely. Learn more →: a vector of raw scores, one per class. To get probabilities, we apply softmaxConverts a vector of real numbers into probabilities summing to 1. Larger inputs get exponentially more probability. Learn more →.

Say the teacher produces logits [4.5, 2.1, 0.3, 1.8, −0.5] for classes: cat, dog, car, bird, fish. At T=1, standard softmax outputs roughly [0.85, 0.08, 0.01, 0.06, 0.01]. The model is 85% confident it is a cat. Everything else is near zero. This is a sharp probability distribution, not a hard label: it gives the answer, but it hides most of the richer relational structure.

Hinton's insight: divide logits by a temperature $T$ before softmax:

p_i = exp(z_i / T) / Σ_j exp(z_j / T)

At higher $T$, the distribution softens. At $T{=}5$, the same logits yield roughly [0.33, 0.21, 0.14, 0.19, 0.12]. Now the model says: "mostly cat, but also somewhat dog-like and bird-like." These soft labels encode the teacher's understanding of inter-class similarity. Hinton called this dark knowledge: information that one-hot hard labels discard.

Why this matters

Soft labels reveal what the teacher learned about similarity: that cats are somewhat dog-like and a bit bird-like, but nothing like cars. This relational knowledge is invisible in hard labels and is exactly what makes distillation more effective than training from scratch.

The Combined Loss Function

The student trains on two objectives. First: standard cross-entropyMeasures how different two probability distributions are. In classification, it compares predicted probabilities against true labels. Lower = better prediction. Learn more → between the student's hard predictions (T=1) and ground-truth labels. This is $\mathcal{L}_{\text{hard}}$.

Second: KL divergenceKullback-Leibler divergence: measures how one probability distribution diverges from a reference. In distillation, it compares student's and teacher's softened outputs. Learn more → between student's and teacher's softened outputs (both at T). This is $\mathcal{L}_{\text{soft}}$, carrying the dark knowledge.

Hinton combined them:

ℒ = α · ℒ_hard + (1 − α) · T² · ℒ_soft

The $T^2$ factor compensates for gradient scaling: softening by $T$ reduces gradient magnitudes by $1/T^2$, so we multiply back to restore balance. Without it, the teacher's signal would vanish at high temperatures. The parameter $\alpha$ controls the balance:

ℒ hard · 30%
T²·ℒ soft · 70%
0.30

In practice, α is tuned empirically, and many successful setups give the soft-target term substantial weight.

Training Schemes

Offline: Teacher is pre-trained and frozen. Soft labels are generated once, then the student trains against them. This is the standard approach (DistilBERT, TinyBERT, most production systems).

Online: Teacher and student train simultaneously. Zhang et al. (2018) showed two networks teaching each other (Deep Mutual Learning) can outperform either alone. No pre-trained teacher needed.

Self-distillation: A model distills from itself. Deeper layers teach shallower ones. This improves generalization even without an external teacher, suggesting that learning from soft distributions itself has a regularizing effect.

Distillation vs. Pruning vs. Quantization

QuantizationReducing numerical precision of weights (float32 → int8). Up to 4x size reduction with minimal accuracy loss and near-zero training cost. Learn more →: Reduce precision. Up to 4x smaller, minimal cost. Start here.

PruningRemoving redundant weights, neurons, or layers. Structured pruning removes whole units; unstructured zeros individual weights. Learn more →: Remove redundant weights/layers. Moderate speedup, needs fine-tuning.

Distillation: Train an entirely new smaller model from a teacher. Highest flexibility and compression, highest training cost.

The Capacity Gap

A student that is too small cannot absorb the teacher's knowledge. This is the capacity gap, studied extensively in Sanh et al. (2019) and Jiao et al. (2020). Research suggests that the teacher-student ratio matters a lot: DistilBERT uses a 2:1 ratio (12→6 layers), while TinyBERT pushes to 3:1 (12→4) with extra transfer objectives. At aggressive compression levels, performance can collapse rather than degrade gracefully. Cho & Hariharan (2019) showed that a stronger teacher can actually hurt a weak student, a counterintuitive finding that highlights the importance of matching capacity.

Limitations and Open Questions

Architecture mismatch. Response-based distillation works across different architectures (transformer to CNN) since it only matches final outputs. But feature-based distillation requires matching intermediate layers, which breaks down when internal representations have different dimensions and structure. Projection layers can bridge this gap at the cost of added complexity.

Data requirements. Standard distillation requires running the teacher on a large dataset to generate soft labels, which is expensive for very large models. Data-free methods exist (using generative models to synthesize training data), but they consistently produce weaker students. The choice of transfer dataset significantly affects final quality.

IP and legal. Distilling from a proprietary model effectively extracts its learned knowledge into your model. This raises unresolved questions about intellectual property, licensing, and whether distillation constitutes derivative use of the teacher's training data, particularly for commercial LLMs.

Safety degradation. Distilled models may not preserve the teacher's alignment properties. A teacher carefully fine-tuned to refuse harmful requests can produce a student that loses some of those guardrails. The relationship between distillation and safety alignment is poorly understood and increasingly important.

Practitioner note

Distillation is not free. Validate distilled models on all properties that matter, not just accuracy: safety, fairness, calibration, out-of-distribution robustness.

What Should You Distill?

Select your scenario for a recommended approach:

Two-stage distillation + quantization

Response-based KD for a compact student, then post-training INT8 quantization. For transformers, TinyBERT-style attention transfer maximizes compression.

Expected: 5-10x smaller, 3-9x faster, 95-97% quality retained.

Read: DistilBERT, TinyBERT

Response-based distillation

Standard Hinton KD into a 2-4x smaller student. Combine with pruning (Minitron-style) to start from teacher weights.

Expected: 2-4x cost reduction with minimal quality loss.

Read: Hinton et al. 2015, Minitron

Reasoning-data distillation

Collect high-quality teacher-generated reasoning examples and use them as supervised fine-tuning data for smaller base models. The student learns a process, not just a label mapping.

Expected: Multi-step reasoning can transfer, but budget a substantial curated reasoning dataset.

Read: DeepSeek-R1

Quantize first, then distill

INT8/INT4 for fastest wins. If more needed, distill into a shallower architecture. FitNets preserves quality in very shallow students.

Expected: Quant alone: 2-4x. Combined: up to 10x.

Read: FitNets
